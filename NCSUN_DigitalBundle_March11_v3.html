Who we are

NCSUN and why today happened.

NCSUN — Norwich Community Support Umbrella Network — is a Community Interest Company built on one founding principle: lived experience is core expertise. Not a nice addition. Not a consultation tick-box. The actual foundation.

We started with a cupboard, a community, and a question nobody was answering: why do people who need support keep falling through the gaps between services that are supposed to catch them? Twenty months later, we have a partial answer. It is not because the services don't exist. It is because the systems that are supposed to connect them don't work, and the people closest to that failure have never been asked to help fix it.

Today was an attempt to change that. You brought your professional expertise. We brought six accounts of what it looks like when the system fails the same person, repeatedly, across five different service areas. Together, that is something neither of us could produce alone.

The six accounts

What you worked with
at the stations.

You have already met these stories. They are here so you can return to them, share them, and carry them into whatever comes next. Every account was processed through the GINE protocol — names, locations, and identifying details removed. What remains is the systemic pattern.

Community Anchor
A new build on the outskirts. The nearest shop half a mile away. Nearly didn't send the email.
+
"I sat with someone for well over an hour and sobbed my heart out. I felt pathetic asking for help but they just listened and they were so kind and so human about it that it did not feel like a transaction at all. They bent their own rules for me that day because our postcode was outside their area but they registered us anyway."

A family of three — one parent with limited mobility, one autistic child, no transport, first week in a new property on the outskirts of the city. None of what followed — British Red Cross, Your Own Place CIC, St Giles Trust, a monthly money management course — starts without a single email to The Feed. An email she nearly didn't send.

The systemic gap: every referral in this account came through one discretionary act of kindness from someone who bent the rules. There is no version of this story that works without that.

Legal Integrity
Four years. Two voices. The system was not built for either of them.
+
"The court said I needed an assessment to work out what help I would need to follow what was happening. They did the assessment. The help they said I needed never happened."
"I did not know bundles were supposed to be under 350 pages. Mine was 669 pages. I did not know because nobody told me and I could not afford a solicitor. The barrister for the local authority found me before we went in and told me the bundle was good."

Two people. Two separate family law cases running at the same time. Both neurodivergent. Neither able to afford legal representation. One had an intermediary assessment that was then ignored. One taught herself enough law to produce a bundle a barrister complimented — and was told by the judge it was all over the place.

The system did not fail them because they were incapable. It failed them because it was not built for them.

LDC Centre
From pantry member to second year LLB. The route exists. It is just not signposted.
+
"It was heartbreaking. I had been earning my own money. I had been good at it. And now I was back on benefits, supporting my children, knowing what I was capable of and not being able to do anything with it."

A celebrant business started in 2013. A breast cancer diagnosis in 2015, a week before her twenty-eighth birthday. A restart attempt in 2019. Lockdown. A £150 HMRC fine. Then, in 2024, a pantry membership at St Giles Trust that led to an NVQ, then Open Learn, then an Access to Law course, then the first year of an LLB. Now midway through her second year.

She did not find her route through a careers service or a structured pathway. She found it through a food pantry. That is not a design feature. It is a consequence of the right person being in the right place at the right time.

"I am still not sure [the system] does now" — on whether it has options for people like her.

Ethical Finance
£195 out of a maintenance loan. The true cost ran into the thousands. None of it counts.
+
"Nobody paid me to do any of it. I used my own money because the alternative was it not happening at all. The food and everything else came to £195. If the hours I had put in had been charged at any kind of professional rate the true cost would have run into the thousands."

With one week's notice: a venue found, materials designed, food sourced, fourteen people in the room, twenty-eight Google Form responses before it had finished. A community event that demonstrably served the public good, run on a maintenance loan and a Universal Credit top-up, with no mechanism to reimburse a single penny of it.

Community infrastructure does not appear from nowhere. Somebody builds it. And right now the people most likely to see what is needed and act on it are often the ones least able to absorb the cost of doing so.

Ethical Technology
31 tablets a day. Brain fog. A prompt she wrote herself. A route no service designed.
+
"To be honest, I did a better job of clearing out my own bank account when I was in crisis than any hacker would have done."

In late 2022: 31 tablets a day, four rounds of medication for scoliosis, degenerative disc disease and cancer-related fatigue, all interacting with each other. Limited use of one leg. Constant brain fog. No support network. Ad hoc delivery driving that did not cover the bills.

In 2024 she did a money management course and started using AI tools to understand her own finances. She put in a prompt that her data should not be retained. She did it every time. It worked.

The tools existed. The bridge to them did not. She built the bridge herself. The gap between the person and the tool that could help them is what GINE exists to map and close.

GINE · The database
Station 6 was different. There was no individual account.
+

There was a printed list of 24 services, organisations and community groups across Norwich and Norfolk. Built entirely from publicly available information, cross-referenced across multiple sources. And there were sticky notes.

The task: if your organisation was not on the list, write its name on a sticky note and put it on the board. If a service was missing, name it. Every sticky note becomes a new row in the GINE database — 423 rows of intelligence gathered across eight Norwich districts, mapping services, referral pathways, and the gaps people fall through.

What you added today is already part of it.

2026 programme

What today was
the beginning of.

Today was the first in a programme running throughout 2026, alternating between Mustard Share Exchange events for professionals and Community Collective events open to all. Every community event feeds directly into the next professional forum. Every forum shapes what we take back to the community. All events fall on the 11th.

The destination: January 2027

A Quarterly Round Table — the first official step toward what Norwich could become, including the first formal discussions regarding a future Poverty Truth Norwich Commission. Everything gathered across 2026 feeds into that table, anonymised, evidenced, and actionable. Today is one of the first bricks.

What comes next

What could we
co-create?

We are not asking for charity. We are asking whether what you saw today is something your organisation wants to be part of building. There are four specific ways that could work.

Volunteers

Individual volunteers, social media live-connecting, or corporate volunteering arrangements. Skills-based or presence-based — both matter.

Space & resources

Reciprocal agreements for venue space and resources. We bring the community. You provide the room.

Formal partnership

A running story and article in NCSUN's magazines, published quarterly from January 2027. Quarter page £100/year. Half page £200/year. Full page £400/year.

Micro organisations currently spend an estimated £750/year on standalone print. Mid-sized charities £1,500–£3,000. Partners save 86–95% on print costs. Every pound saved goes back into your frontline. Every partnership is co-created — no fixed template, no one-size-fits-all.

Lived experience consultancy

We completed our first consultancy pilots as a community group before registration — six consultants, six weeks, eight actionable recommendations, four still in use, independently valued at £6,000.

Over the next six months we are looking to partner with organisations to run further pilots. Each pilot helps us build the evidence base to move our volunteers into paid part-time or ad hoc roles. If you would like to be one of those organisations, that conversation starts now.

If today has prompted thinking about what any of this could look like for your organisation, that conversation starts with Charli.
